$\diamond$ \, Real and Imaginary parts of a complex number:

Let $z=a+bi$.  Then the {\bf real and imaginary parts of $z$} are denoted as
\begin{center}
	$Re(z) = a$ \hspace{1.0cm} \text{and} \hspace{1.0cm} $Im(z) = b$	
\end{center}

\vspace{0.2cm}
 
$\diamond$ \, {\bf Euler's Formula}: \hspace{0.5cm} $\D{e^{i\theta} = \cos \theta + i \sin \theta}$

\vspace{0.2cm}

$\diamond$ \, These ideas lead to the following very important identities for $\cos \theta$ and $\sin \theta$.
$$
\cos\theta = Re(e^{i\theta}) = \frac{e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta}}{2} \hspace{1.0cm} \text{and} \hspace{1.0cm} 
\sin\theta = Im(e^{i\theta}) = \frac{e^{i\theta}-e^{-i\theta}}{2i}
$$
$\diamond$ \, Euler's formula gives another way to view the polar form of a complex number.  

Recall that $z=a+bi$ can be written in the following {\bf polar form}
$$
z=r(\cos \theta + i \sin \theta)	
$$
where $a=r\cos \theta$,  $b = r\sin \theta$, and $r = |z|=\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$

In terms of Euler's formula, any complex number can therefore be written in the form
$$
z = |z|e^{i\theta}
$$
$\diamond$ \, Useful trig identities
$$
\cos(-\theta) = \cos(\theta) \hspace{1.0cm} \text{and} \hspace{1.0cm} \sin(-\theta) = -\sin(\theta)
$$

\UL{Example 1}: \,\, Write each of the following complex numbers in standard form ($a+bi$)

\vspace{0.2cm}

(a) $\D{e^{-i\pi/4}}$

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.2cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \D{e^{-i\pi/4} =} & \D{e^{i(-\pi/4)}} \\
	&=&\D{\cos (-\pi/4) + i \sin (-\pi/4)}\\
\end{array}
$

(b) $\D{e^{2e^{3+i\pi/6}}}$

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.2cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \D{2e^{3+i\pi/6} =} & \D{2e^{3}e^{i\pi/6}} \\
	&=&\D{2e^{3}\left[\cos (\pi/6) + i \sin (\pi/6)\right]}\\
	&=&\D{2e^{3}\cos (\pi/6) + i \,2e^{3}\sin (\pi/6)}\\
\end{array}
$

(c) $\D{e^{e^{i}}}$

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.2cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \D{e^{e^{i}} =} & \D{e^{\cos (1) + i \sin (1)}} \\
	&=&\D{e^{\cos (1)} e^{i \sin (1)}} \\
	&=&\D{e^{\cos (1)} [\cos (\sin (1)) + i \sin (\sin (1))]} \\
	&=&\D{e^{\cos (1)} \cos (\sin (1)) + i \, e^{\cos (1)}\sin (\sin (1))} \\
\end{array}
$

(d) $\D{\frac{e^{3i}-e^{-3i}}{2i}}$

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.4cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \D{\frac{e^{3i}-e^{-3i}}{2i} =} & \D{\frac{(\cos (3) + i \sin (3)) - (\cos (-3) + i \sin (-3))}{2i}} \\
	&=&\D{\frac{[\cos (3) + i \sin (3)] - [\cos (3) - i \sin (3)]}{2i}} \\
	&=&\D{\frac{ 2 i \sin (3)}{2i}} \\
	&=&\D{\sin(3) = \sin(3) + 0i} \\
\end{array}
$

\UL{Example 2}: \,\, Write each of the following complex numbers in polar form ($re^{i\theta}$)

\vspace{0.2cm}

(a) $\D{\frac{1-i}{3}}$ 

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.4cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \multicolumn{2}{l}{\text{Note this point would be in the 4th quadrant}} \\
	& \D{r =} & \D{\left|\frac{1-i}{3}\right| = \left|\frac{1}{3}-\frac{i}{3}\right| = \sqrt{\frac{1}{9}+\frac{1}{9}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{9}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}} \\
	& \D{\theta =} & \D{\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\frac{1}{3}}{-\frac{1}{3}}\right) = \tan^{-1}(-1) = \frac{3\pi}{4} \,\,\text{or}\,\, -\frac{\pi}{4} } \\
	& \multicolumn{2}{l}{\text{Since $\theta$ must be in the 4th quadrant $\quad \implies \quad \theta = -\frac{\pi}{4}$ }} \\
	& \text{Therefore} & \quad \D{\frac{1-i}{3} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{3}e^{-i\pi/4}} \\
\end{array}
$

(b) $\D{(1+i)^6}$ 

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.4cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \multicolumn{2}{l}{\text{Lets convert $1+i$ first. (It is in the 1st quadrant)}} \\
	& \D{r =} & \D{\left|1+i\right| = \sqrt{1+1} = \sqrt{2} } \\
	& \D{\theta =} & \D{\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{1}\right) = \tan^{-1}(1) = \frac{\pi}{4} \,\,\text{or}\,\, -\frac{\pi}{4} } \\
	& \multicolumn{2}{l}{\text{Since $\theta$ must be in the 1st quadrant $\quad \implies \quad \theta = \frac{\pi}{4}$ }} \\
	& \text{Therefore} & \quad \D{(1+i)^6 = \left(\sqrt{2}e^{i\pi/4}\right)^6 = (\sqrt{2})^6e^{i\,6\pi/4}= 8e^{i\,3\pi/2}} \\
\end{array}
$

(c) $\D{(1+i)^{1/2}}$ 

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.4cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \multicolumn{2}{l}{\text{From (b), we have \,\, }\D{ 1+i = \sqrt{2}e^{i\pi/4} }} \\
	& \text{Therefore} & \quad \D{(1+i)^{1/2} = \left(2^{1/2}e^{i\pi/4}\right)^{1/2} = 2^{1/4}\left(e^{i\,\pi/4}\right)^{1/2} = 2^{1/4} e^{i(\pi/8+2\pi k/2)} \quad k=0,1} \\
	&& \D{k=0: \quad 2^{1/4}e^{i(\pi/8)}} \\
	&& \D{k=1: \quad 2^{1/4}e^{i(9\pi/8)}} \\
\end{array}
$

(d) $\D{\frac{2i}{3e^{4+i}}}$ 

$
\setlength{\extrarowheight}{0.4cm}
\begin{array}{rrl}
	Solution: & \D{\frac{2i}{3e^{4+i}} = \frac{2e^{i\pi/2}}{3e^4e^{i}} = \frac{2}{3e^4}e^{i\pi/2-i} = \frac{2}{3e^4}e^{i(\pi/2-1)}} \\
\end{array}
$
